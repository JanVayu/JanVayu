# How Farmers Outsmart Satellites: The Stubble Burning Shell Game

**Published:** 28 March 2026 | **Author:** Team JanVayu | **Reading time:** 4 min

---

Every October, as the kharif paddy harvest ends across Punjab and Haryana, a familiar ritual begins. Farmers set fire to crop residue to clear fields for the winter wheat sowing. Smoke drifts southeast into the Indo-Gangetic plain, and Delhi's air quality collapses. Governments announce crackdowns. Satellite images circulate on social media. And then, quietly, the fires adapt.

A NASA Earth Observatory analysis published in 2025 revealed something remarkable: between 2020 and 2024, the peak timing of stubble fires in Punjab and Haryana shifted from approximately 1:30 PM to 5:00 PM. The reason was straightforward. Farmers had learned when polar-orbiting satellites — the primary detection tool used by ISRO and IARI — passed overhead, and they were burning later in the day to avoid detection.

## The Numbers

The scale of the problem is significant but contested. Under the ISRO-IARI satellite monitoring protocol, Punjab recorded 10,207 wheat harvest fire events between April and May 2025. Haryana reported 1,832 incidents. These numbers represent a substantial reduction from peak years — Punjab and Haryana collectively recorded more than a 90 percent reduction in fire incidences during the 2025 paddy season compared to the same period in 2022.

But the satellite data tells an incomplete story. The fires detected represent only those visible to satellites at the moment of overpass. A study published in Air Quality, Atmosphere & Health in 2026, using geostationary satellite data (MSG) that observes continuously rather than at fixed intervals, found that the true number of fires is likely higher than what polar-orbiting satellites capture — particularly as farmers adjust their burning windows.

## The Government Response

The response has been expensive and multi-pronged. Between 2018-19 and 2025-26, the central government released Rs 4,237.47 crore to address crop residue burning. States distributed more than 3.53 lakh crop residue management machines to individual farmers and established over 43,535 Custom Hiring Centres where farmers can rent equipment to manage stubble without burning.

The Commission for Air Quality Management (CAQM), the statutory body overseeing NCR air quality, has mandated state-level action plans for each burning season. After the 10,207 fire events recorded in the 2025 wheat season, CAQM ordered time-bound execution of revised plans for the 2026 cycle with stricter enforcement protocols.

Yet the fundamental economics have not changed. A Happy Seeder — the primary machine alternative to burning — costs approximately Rs 1.5 to 2.5 lakh. Burning costs nothing. For marginal farmers operating on thin margins with small landholdings, the arithmetic is simple. Even with subsidies and free equipment access through Custom Hiring Centres, burning remains faster, easier, and cheaper in the short window between harvest and sowing.

## The Health Toll

The contribution of stubble burning to Delhi's winter pollution crisis is well-documented but often overstated in public discourse. CREA analysis of source apportionment data suggests that stubble burning contributes 25 to 40 percent of Delhi's PM2.5 during peak burning weeks in October and November. Vehicular emissions, industrial sources, construction dust, and waste burning account for the majority of pollution year-round.

This distinction matters for policy. Stubble burning is a seasonal contributor that receives disproportionate political and media attention, in part because it originates in other states and allows Delhi's government to deflect responsibility. The capital's own pollution sources — which it controls — receive less scrutiny.

That said, the stubble burning contribution during peak weeks is severe enough to push air quality from "very poor" to "severe" or "severe plus" categories, triggering GRAP Stage III and IV restrictions, school closures, and construction bans. The fires convert a chronic crisis into an acute emergency.

## What Satellite Evasion Reveals

The timing shift documented by NASA is, in its way, a tribute to human adaptability. But it reveals a deeper problem with India's enforcement model. When the primary monitoring tool can be defeated by waiting a few hours, the enforcement framework is fundamentally fragile.

More effective approaches would include continuous monitoring via geostationary satellites (which India's INSAT fleet could theoretically provide), ground-level enforcement through patwaris and block-level officers, economic incentives that make alternatives genuinely cheaper than burning, and — most importantly — addressing the underlying crop calendar pressure that leaves farmers with a narrow window and no economically viable alternative.

India has spent over four thousand crore rupees and distributed hundreds of thousands of machines. The fires have reduced significantly. But the cat-and-mouse game with satellites shows that the problem has been managed, not solved. As long as burning remains the rational economic choice for millions of farmers, the fires will continue — they will just burn at different times.

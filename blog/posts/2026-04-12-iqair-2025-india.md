# IQAir 2025: India's Air Got Worse

**Published:** 12 April 2026 | **Author:** Team JanVayu | **Reading time:** 4 min

---

On March 24, 2026, IQAir released its eighth annual World Air Quality Report — the most comprehensive global assessment of PM2.5 pollution, covering 9,446 cities across 143 countries. The findings for India are unambiguous: the crisis is deepening, not retreating.

## Loni Is Now the Most Polluted City on Earth

The city of Loni in Uttar Pradesh recorded an annual average PM2.5 concentration of 112.5 micrograms per cubic metre in 2025. That is a 23% increase from the previous year and more than 22 times the WHO annual guideline of 5 micrograms per cubic metre. Loni displaces Byrnihat, Meghalaya, which held the unwelcome distinction in the 2024 report.

This is not a statistical anomaly. Loni sits in the Delhi-NCR airshed, a region where vehicular emissions, construction dust, industrial output, and seasonal crop burning converge to produce some of the highest sustained PM2.5 readings anywhere on the planet. The fact that a city adjacent to India's capital now leads the global ranking is a policy failure with a precise address.

## Global Compliance Is Falling

The report found that only 14% of cities worldwide met the WHO annual PM2.5 guideline of 5 micrograms per cubic metre, down from 17% the previous year. Only thirteen countries and territories met the standard at the national level: French Polynesia, Puerto Rico, the U.S. Virgin Islands, Barbados, New Caledonia, Iceland, Bermuda, Reunion, Andorra, Australia, Grenada, Panama, and Estonia.

India's national average PM2.5 concentration stood at 48.9 micrograms per cubic metre — nearly ten times the WHO limit. The country ranked among the most polluted globally, alongside Pakistan (67.3), Bangladesh (66.1), Tajikistan (57.3), and Chad (53.6).

## The Monitoring Gap

One of the report's most alarming findings has nothing to do with pollution levels. In March 2025, the United States State Department ended its global air quality monitoring programme at embassies and consulates. This programme had provided independent, publicly accessible PM2.5 readings in dozens of countries — often serving as a check on official government data.

The Centre for Research on Energy and Clean Air (CREA) reported that monitoring efforts were weakened in 44 countries, and six were left without any monitoring at all. For India, where citizens have long questioned the accuracy and placement of official CPCB monitoring stations, the loss of independent verification makes accountability harder.

## What the Science Says

The IQAir report landed alongside two landmark studies in The Lancet Planetary Health that, for the first time, established causal links between PM2.5 exposure and mortality in India using rigorous econometric methods rather than correlational analysis.

The first study, using a difference-in-differences approach, estimated 1.5 million additional deaths per year compared to conditions where India met WHO guidelines. The second, a multi-city causal modelling study covering ten Indian cities, confirmed significant short-term mortality effects from daily PM2.5 exposure.

A separate study in Science Advances demonstrated that whatever air quality improvements India has achieved in recent years have been distributed unequally. Wealthier urban areas have seen modest gains; poorer and rural regions have seen none.

## What This Means

India's air pollution problem is not improving at the pace needed to protect human life. The IQAir data shows concentrations rising in many cities even as the National Clean Air Programme approaches its 2026 deadline with most cities far from their targets.

The numbers reinforce the case for JanVayu's core premise: public memory is a prerequisite for public accountability. If this data is not archived, contextualised, and made accessible to citizens, it cannot drive the policy changes that 1.4 billion people need.

The air does not care about press conferences. It responds only to sustained, measurable intervention — and the 2025 data shows India is not there yet.
